\title{Firm Size, Wages, and Allocative Frictions in Colombia}
\author{Oliver Pardo and Jorge Orozco}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
This paper documents the firm-size wage premium in Colombia using harmonized microdata from the Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares for 2008--2019 and 2021--2025. Larger firms pay substantially higher real hourly wages than solo workers and very small firms, while employment remains concentrated in solo work and micro-firms. Much of the raw wage gradient is associated with differences in workers' observable characteristics, sector-year composition, and job formality. However, the premium remains positive and statistically significant after controlling for these factors: workers in firms with 101 or more employees earn about 35 percent more than otherwise comparable solo workers. The premium is positive throughout the sample, with no statistically conclusive evidence of a declining trend. Because the data do not observe firm productivity or worker-firm matches, this conditional premium should not be interpreted as a causal firm-size effect or as direct proof of misallocation. Instead, we interpret it as a marker of wage inequality and labor-market segmentation that is consistent with allocative frictions limiting firm growth and worker reallocation toward larger, potentially more productive employers.
\end{abstract}
