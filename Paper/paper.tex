\title{Firm Size, Wages, and Allocative Frictions in Colombia}
\author{Oliver Pardo and Jorge Orozco}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Firm-size wage premia are well documented in advanced economies, but their magnitude and interpretation in middle-income labor markets with large informal sectors remain less settled. This paper documents the firm-size wage premium in Colombia using harmonized microdata from the Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares covering 2008 to 2025 (excluding 2020). Real hourly wages rise sharply with firm size: workers in firms with 101 or more employees earn about 35 percent more than otherwise comparable solo workers. Worker observables — gender, education, and sector — explain part of the unconditional premium, but a sizable conditional premium remains. Crucially, the premium also survives controlling for job formality status, indicating that it is not simply a relabeling of the formal–informal wage gap that dominates dual-labor-market accounts of developing-country wages. The premium is stable over the sample period, with no evidence of secular decline. Combined with the thin middle of the Colombian firm-size distribution — few workers are employed in firms with 11 to 100 employees — the premium is consistent with allocative frictions that limit firm growth and worker reallocation toward larger, potentially more productive employers.
\end{abstract}
