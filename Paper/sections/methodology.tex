\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Descriptive Premiums}

We begin by documenting the raw firm-size gradient in real hourly labor income.
For each firm-size group \(g\) and year \(t\), we compute the weighted mean:

\[
  \bar{w}_{gt} =
  \frac{\sum_{i \in (g,t)} fex_{it} w_{it}^{real}}
       {\sum_{i \in (g,t)} fex_{it}},
\]

where \(fex_{it}\) is the GEIH expansion factor. We also compute the employment
share of each firm-size group:

\[
  s_{gt} =
  \frac{\sum_{i \in (g,t)} fex_{it}}
       {\sum_{i \in t} fex_{it}}.
\]

These two objects are used in the descriptive figures: the vertical position of
each point shows real hourly labor income, and the bubble size shows the share of
employment represented by each firm-size group. For the comparison between 2008
and 2025, the descriptive difference for group \(g\) is:

\[
  Diff_g = 100 \times
  \left(\frac{\bar{w}_{g,2025}}{\bar{w}_{g,2008}} - 1\right).
\]

\subsection{Econometric Specification}

The main econometric exercise estimates the conditional firm-size wage premium
in a log-wage model. The baseline specification is:

\[
  \ln(w_{it}^{real}) =
  \alpha
  + \sum_{g \neq g_0} \beta_g \mathbf{1}\{Size_{it}=g\}
  + X_{it}'\gamma
  + \lambda_{s(i) \times t}
  + \varepsilon_{it},
\]

where \(w_{it}^{real}\) is real hourly labor income for worker \(i\) in year
\(t\), \(Size_{it}\) is the harmonized firm-size category, and \(g_0\) is the
reference group. We use solo workers as the reference category. This choice makes
the coefficient path directly interpretable as the wage premium associated with
moving from individual work to increasingly larger firms. Because solo workers
may combine self-employment and very small-scale wage employment, we interpret
the estimates as conditional gaps relative to the bottom of the firm-size
distribution rather than as pure firm effects.

The vector \(X_{it}\) includes a female-worker indicator, education dummies, and
a formal-employment indicator. The fixed effect \(\lambda_{s(i) \times t}\)
absorbs sector-year conditions, allowing each broad sector to have its own wage
level in each year. This is important because large firms are not randomly
distributed across sectors, and sectoral shocks may be correlated with both wages
and firm size. The model is estimated with GEIH expansion weights, and standard
errors are clustered by sector.

In the results table, we report four nested specifications. The first column
includes only firm-size indicators. The second adds sector-year fixed effects.
The third adds gender and education controls, but excludes formality. The fourth
adds formality. Comparing the third and fourth columns shows how much of the
firm-size gradient is absorbed by differences in formal employment across firm
sizes.

The coefficients \(\beta_g\) are log-point differences relative to solo workers.
For interpretation, we transform them into percentage premiums:

\[
  Premium_g = 100 \times \left(\exp(\hat{\beta}_g)-1\right).
\]

Confidence intervals are transformed in the same way:

\[
  CI_g =
  \left[
  100 \times \left(\exp(\hat{\beta}_g - 1.96\,se(\hat{\beta}_g))-1\right),
  100 \times \left(\exp(\hat{\beta}_g + 1.96\,se(\hat{\beta}_g))-1\right)
  \right].
\]

The graphical test of statistical significance is therefore direct: if the 95\%
confidence interval for a firm-size group excludes zero, the conditional premium
is statistically significant at the 5\% level. If the interval crosses zero, the
estimated premium is not statistically distinguishable from zero at conventional
levels.

\subsection{Extensions}

The same framework can be extended to study the evolution of the premium over
time by interacting firm-size categories with year indicators:

\[
  \ln(w_{it}^{real}) =
  \alpha
  + \sum_{g \neq g_0}\sum_t
    \beta_{gt}\mathbf{1}\{Size_{it}=g\}\mathbf{1}\{Year_i=t\}
  + X_{it}'\gamma
  + \lambda_{s(i) \times t}
  + \varepsilon_{it}.
\]

This dynamic specification produces a sequence of firm-size premiums by year and
can be used to assess whether the premium has widened, narrowed, or remained
stable. Additional robustness checks can add region-year or occupation-year
fixed effects once those variables are fully harmonized in the person-level
panel.
