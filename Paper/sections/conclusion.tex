\section{Conclusion}

This paper documents a clear firm-size wage premium in Colombia. The descriptive
evidence shows that real hourly wages rise sharply with firm size: workers in
firms with 101 or more employees earn more than twice the average hourly income
of solo workers. The same descriptive evidence also shows why the econometric
exercise is necessary. Larger firms employ more formal and more educated workers,
so the raw wage premium combines wage-setting differences with worker sorting and
labor-market segmentation.

The regression results show that a substantial conditional premium remains after
controlling for gender, education, formality, and sector-year fixed effects. In
the full specification, the premium for workers in firms with 101 or more
employees is about 35 percent relative to solo workers, and all firm-size
categories above solo work have positive and statistically significant premiums.
At the same time, the reduction in the 101+ coefficient from 1.017 log points in
the raw specification to 0.299 log points in the full specification indicates
that formality, observable skills, and sector-year composition explain a large
share of the raw gap.

These findings are relevant beyond wage inequality. In a labor market with high
informality and a large share of employment in solo work and very small firms,
the firm-size wage premium is also a diagnostic of efficiency and potential
misallocation. If larger firms are more productive or provide better matches,
then barriers that keep workers and firms from scaling may have aggregate
consequences. Future versions of the paper should extend the analysis with
region, occupation, and worker-age controls, and should explore whether the
premium is stronger in sectors where productivity dispersion or informality is
larger.
