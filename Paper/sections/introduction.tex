\section{Introduction}

Large firms tend to pay higher wages than small firms. This empirical regularity,
usually known as the firm-size wage premium, is one of the most robust facts in
labor economics. Classic evidence for the United States shows that the premium
survives controls for many observable worker characteristics, although its
interpretation remains open: it may reflect worker sorting, compensating
differences, efficiency wages, rent sharing, monitoring costs, technology,
internal labor markets, or productivity differences across firms
\citep{brown1989employer,oi1999firm,troske1999evidence}. Evidence outside the
United States also suggests that the size-wage gradient is relevant in settings
where institutions, informality, and firm heterogeneity are central features of
the labor market \citep{velenchik1997government,lehmer2006urban}.

Colombia is a natural setting in which to study this question. The country
combines a highly heterogeneous productive structure with a large informal
sector, strong differences between small and large firms, and persistent wage
inequality. Existing Colombian work has studied wage differentials by sector and
firm characteristics in the formal labor market \citep{iregui2012diferenciales},
but, to our knowledge, there is no systematic documentation of the firm-size wage
premium for the Colombian labor market using nationally representative household
survey data over a long period. This gap matters because firm size is closely
connected to productivity, formality, access to technology, managerial capacity,
and the allocation of workers across the productive structure.

This paper documents the firm-size wage premium in Colombia. The central
question is descriptive and econometric: do workers employed in larger firms earn
higher real hourly labor income than comparable workers employed in smaller
firms, and are those differences statistically significant after controlling for
observable worker characteristics and sector-year conditions? We use harmonized
microdata from the Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares (GEIH) for 2008--2019 and
2021--2025. The analysis focuses on real hourly labor income, firm-size
categories reported in the survey, and a set of harmonized controls for gender,
education, formality, and economic sector.

The contribution is threefold. First, we construct a comparable firm-size
classification across changes in the GEIH questionnaire, including a harmonized
``101+'' category for the largest firms. Second, we present descriptive evidence
on how real hourly labor income varies by firm size, education, sector, gender,
and formality. Third, we estimate conditional firm-size wage premiums and report
confidence intervals, so that the paper distinguishes visually large differences
from differences that are statistically distinguishable from zero. The estimates
should be interpreted as conditional wage gaps rather than causal effects of
working in a larger firm.
