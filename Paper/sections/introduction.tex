\section{Introduction}

Large firms tend to pay higher wages than small firms. This empirical regularity,
usually known as the firm-size wage premium, is one of the classic facts in labor
economics. Its interpretation, however, is not mechanical. The premium may
reflect worker sorting, compensating differentials, monitoring costs, efficiency
wages, internal labor markets, rent sharing, technology, or productivity
differences across firms \citep{brown1989employer,oi1999firm,troske1999evidence}.
The matched employer-employee literature further shows that wages reflect both
worker heterogeneity and firm wage-setting heterogeneity
\citep{abowd1999high,card2013workplace,song2019firming}.

The firm-size wage premium is relevant not only for wage inequality, but also for
efficiency and misallocation. If larger firms pay more because they are more
productive, use better technology, comply with formal labor regulations, or share
rents with workers, then the distribution of employment across firm sizes matters
for aggregate productivity. Conversely, if a large share of workers remains in
solo work and very small firms despite large wage premia in larger firms, the
premium may signal frictions that prevent workers or firms from moving toward
more productive matches. This connects the firm-size wage premium to the broader
development literature on misallocation, informality, and productivity dispersion
\citep{restuccia2008policy,hsieh2009misallocation,meghir2015wages,reed2019large}.

Colombia is a natural setting in which to study this question. The country
combines a highly heterogeneous productive structure with a large informal
sector, strong differences between small and large firms, and persistent wage
inequality. Existing Colombian work has studied wage differentials by sector and
firm characteristics in the formal labor market \citep{iregui2012diferenciales},
but there is little systematic evidence on the firm-size wage premium using
nationally representative household survey data over a long period. This gap
matters because firm size is closely connected to formality, access to
technology, managerial capacity, regulatory compliance, and the allocation of
workers across the productive structure.

This paper documents the firm-size wage premium in Colombia using harmonized
microdata from the Gran Encuesta Integrada de Hogares (GEIH) for 2008--2019 and
2021--2025. The central question is descriptive and econometric: do workers in
larger firms earn higher real hourly labor income than comparable workers in solo
work or smaller firms, and how much of the raw gradient remains after accounting
for observable worker characteristics and sector-year conditions?

The contribution is threefold. First, we construct a comparable firm-size
classification across changes in the GEIH questionnaire, including a harmonized
``101+'' category for the largest firms. Second, we show that the raw firm-size
gradient is steep and tightly connected to worker composition: larger firms have
much higher rates of formality and higher education. Third, we estimate
conditional firm-size wage premiums and confidence intervals. The estimates
should be interpreted as conditional wage gaps rather than causal effects of
working in a larger firm, but they provide a useful diagnostic of wage inequality,
labor-market segmentation, and potential misallocation in Colombia.
