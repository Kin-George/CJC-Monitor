\section{Descriptive Statistics}

Before estimating the econometric specification, we document the raw structure of
the sample. The purpose of these descriptive statistics is to show how employment,
wages, and worker characteristics vary across firm-size categories. This is
important because the conditional premium estimated below combines two empirical
facts: larger firms pay higher raw wages, and workers in larger firms also differ
systematically in formality and education.

The descriptive patterns reveal four facts that motivate the regression
analysis. First, employment is concentrated in low-scale units. In the pooled
regression sample, solo workers and firms with up to ten workers account for
65.3 percent of weighted employment; in 2025 they still account for 61.3 percent.
Second, the middle of the firm-size distribution is thin: firms with 11--100
workers account for only 12.3 percent of pooled employment. Third, the largest
harmonized category is economically important: firms with 101 or more workers
account for 22.5 percent of pooled employment, rising from 18.8 percent in 2008
to 25.2 percent in 2025. Fourth, the raw wage gradient is steep: average real
hourly income in the 101+ category is more than two and a half times the average
among solo workers.

Taken together, these facts point to a segmented employment structure: a large
mass of workers is located in solo work and micro-firms, a relatively small share
is located in intermediate firm-size categories, and a sizeable minority is in
large firms. This pattern is relevant for the interpretation of the wage premium.
If large firms pay more because they are more productive or offer better matches,
the concentration of employment at the bottom is consistent with an allocative
friction. If, instead, the premium mainly reflects worker sorting into larger and
more formal firms, the same pattern is primarily a distributional and
segmentation fact. The econometric exercise below is designed to separate part of
these channels by controlling for observed worker characteristics, formality, and
sector-year conditions; it cannot, however, directly measure marginal products or
fully identify misallocation.

Worker composition varies sharply with firm size. Formality and higher education
both rise monotonically with firm size, which means that raw wage differences
partly reflect observable worker sorting across firms.
