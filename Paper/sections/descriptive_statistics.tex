\section{Descriptive Statistics}

Before estimating the econometric specification, we document the raw structure of
the sample. The purpose of these descriptive statistics is to show how employment,
wages, and worker characteristics vary across firm-size categories. This is
important because the conditional premium estimated below combines two empirical
facts: larger firms pay higher raw wages, and workers in larger firms also differ
systematically in formality and education.

The descriptive patterns reveal three facts that motivate the regression
analysis. First, solo workers remain the largest category in the labor market,
but employment in firms with 101 or more workers increased from 18.8 percent in
2008 to 25.2 percent in 2025. Second, the raw wage gradient is steep: average
real hourly income in the 101+ category is more than twice the average among
solo workers. Third, worker composition varies sharply with firm size. Formality
and higher education both rise monotonically with firm size, which means that
raw wage differences partly reflect observable worker sorting across firms.
